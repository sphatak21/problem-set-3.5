A Basic Guide to Sleepers, Breakouts, and Busts

A sleeper is a player that well outperforms his ADP, or average draft position. The sleeper is the epitomy of what fantasy football is about: the name of the game is to compile the lineup that has the greatest value for the price paid. A sleeper last year could have been Tarik Cohen. The Bears running back was virtually an unknown, as Jordan Howard had dominated the backfield the year before. However, Cohen was used as a change of pace back and allowed the fairly one demensional Bears offense to be a little more versatile. Sleepers are difficult to predict, because there are several different factors that can affect the potential work that a player gets. For example, sleepers can be made because of circumstances such as suspension, demonstrated by the emergence of wide reciever Juju Smith-Schuster of the Pittsburgh Steelers. Smith-Schuster was called in after reciever Martavius Bryant was reactivated aftesr a long suspension. Although Bryant was still available much of the season, Smith-Schuster was able to solidify himself as the WR2 of the Steelers (behind Antonio Brown) after a string of solid performances.

A breakout player is similar to a sleeper, but a sleeper that "breaks out" tot the upper echelon of fantasy players. Predicting breakouts is even more difficult than predicting a sleeper, because a player must surpass his ADP and be a top 10, if not top 5 player. A player that demonstrated this last year was rookie running back Kareem Hunt. After exploding in Week 1 versus the New England Patriots, rushing for 148 yards and a touchdown and adding 98 recieving yards off of 5 catches, 2 of which were touchdowns, Hunt continued his rampage by becoming the NFL leader in rushing that season. Rookies such as Hunt are generally candidates to become elite players, especially if they are thrust into a huge role because of an injury. A breakout player is generally also created because of high volume. A breakout player must be able to sustain his success over more than one game. This is the most important feature of a breakout player. He is able to sustain his elite performances over the entire season, and eventually can become a league winner for your team

A bust is a player that comes into the season with high expectations but significantly underperforms his ADP. This can happen for several reasons: the player can get injured, but still play, only to have that injury linger and negatively impact his play. Busts can also be players that have tons of talent but are may be limited by another player in his position, or just cannot get into the endzone for his life. Out of every draft, it is critical to limit the number of busts that are on your team. Busts are often high risk-high reward players. Terrele Pryor Sr. was an example of a bust. After being a sleeper in the 2016 season for the abysmal Cleveland Browns, there were hopes that he would thrive as the number one reciever on the Redskins, an offense that was primed for a big season with young and improving quarterback Kirk Cousins under center. As it turned out, those who drafter Pryor were disappointed, as he was terrible, and barely made the team by the end of the season.
