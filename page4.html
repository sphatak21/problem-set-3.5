Quarterbacks

The quarterback is arguably the most important position on the football and on your fantasy football team. He, generally, scores the most points and has the biggest impact on the game. It is not a stretch to equate the quality of your quarterback to the quality of your team: a better quarterback means a better team. In standard 10 team leagues, draft at most two quarterbacks, especially if the second one has a good matchup during your starting quarterback’s bye week. Drafting more than two is a waste of valuable roster space: drafting one is risky but a waiver wire pick up can still be sufficient for a bye week fill in.

Running Backs

In standard 10 team leagues, there are two running back positions, with an option of flexing one (we will revisit the flex position later). It is essential to grab a running back early in your draft in general, as the running back position is not very deep most years. Think about it like this: in 32 NFL teams, only about 20 have good offenses, and only 15-20 have running backs that are starter caliber, and out of them anywhere from between 3-5 will go in the first round. This does not even account for the possibility that a running back may be a “bust” or, due to the physicality of the position, become injured. The first and second round itself depletes the draft of many high quality running backs. It is important in a standard draft to take at least 3 running backs, or four if there is a sleeper available.

Wide Receivers

In standard 10 team leagues, the wide receiver is the position where the most gambling occurs. Traditionally, the receiver gets .1 points per receiving yard, and in points-per-reception play, (PPR) every time a player catches a ball the player gets a point. There are the reliable, 75 yards per game on 6-8 receptions with maybe a touchdown here and there; basically the Jarvis Landry’s of the world. And then there are the the Odell Beckham’s, Antonio Browns of the world who record 100+ yards a game with a scope of anywhere from no touchdowns to a ridiculous amount. These are the creme de la creme, and should definitely be targeted in the first or second round of your draft; again, depending on your position. They key to a successful wide receiver in fantasy football is volume. There will always be the DeSean Jacksons of the world, the players who erupt with 180 yards with two touchdowns off of 3 receptions one game, then have 1 reception for 10 yards the next game. These are not the receivers ideal for starting for your roster. The consistently targeted in throughout the game and especially in the red zone (near the endzone) are the players that you should target. Similarly to running backs, there are two slots for receivers and you can flex (again, later). Carry at least three receivers.

Tight Ends

The tight end position is never deep, as most passing attacks are facilitated through the wide receivers and running backs. However, the important trait of a premium tight end is how many touchdowns he scores. Since the position is never deep, it is okay (unless you are going for a star such as Travis Kelce, Rob Gronkowski, or Zach Ertz) to draft a good tight end for a lower ADP than to buy too high and miss out on another skill player. Carry only one tight end, and pick one off of the waiver wire when your starter is on bye.

Flex

The Flex, short for flexible, is named this for the reason that you can place a running back, wide receiver, or a tight end here (some platforms call it a W/R/T position). The flex is where you can get creative, as you can alternate between players that are in form and out of form, players that have good matchups with those who have poor matchups, and those who have the volatility to become fantasy league winners with busts. Indeed the flex is the most important unimportant position in fantasy, depending on who you put there. It can be the next Alvin Kamara or it could be Terrelle Pryor. The flex position is ideally suited for any of a manager’s needs.

D/ST

D/ST stands for Defense/Special Teams. Don’t overthink it. Draft one in the later rounds of your draft, and swap it out for another one during that team’s bye week. Although defenses can have monster games from time to time, it is incredibly hard to predict, and time and a high draft pick should not be wasted on trying to pick the best one available. Do not overthink it.

Kickers

Same as D/ST. Do not overthink it. Try to land a kicker on an offense with trouble in the red zone, because he will have many opportunities to score field goals. The last round is generally devoted to kickers. Again, draft only one and swap it out for another one during a bye week.
